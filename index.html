About Kintsukuroi

Photography as a way of tracing cracks — the fractures in people, cities, bodies, time — and filling them with light instead of hiding them. Each image is a small act of repair, a quiet celebration of the places where life did not go as planned, but still found a way to shine through.

This is not a gallery of perfection. It is a room full of almosts, not‑yets, and still‑heres, held together with the gold of attention.

Bring me a cup of tea

Memento Mori — a story in one room

Bring me a cup of tea..

I am glad you exist.

“You gave meaning to my life,” he said, as he carefully wrapped both hands around the warm ceramic cup. His eyes were tired, yet lucid. Full of something I couldn’t name.

I nodded slowly, the way you do when words feel too heavy to carry.

“I was never afraid of dying,” he continued. “But I was terrified of leaving before I mattered.”

Outside, the light was doing that thing it does in autumn — slanting gold through thin curtains, making everything look like memory before it even becomes one.

“Do you think a person can feel when they’ve done enough?” he asked.

I thought about it. Really thought about it.

“I think,” I said, “some people feel it in their chest. Like a settling. Like the last note of a song that didn’t need a longer ending.”

He smiled. Not the polite kind. The real kind. The kind that lives somewhere behind the eyes.

“That’s exactly it,” he said. “That’s exactly what this feels like.”

We sat for a while after that. The tea went cold. Neither of us moved to warm it.

There are moments that don’t ask to be remembered — they simply stay.

I nodded, as if I understood him clearly, and looked back out the window.

He took another sip of the tea and a defiant smile emerged.
